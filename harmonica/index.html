Triangular Harmonica Notation

A Proof of Concept by G.K. Farmer

The term "tablature" derives from the Latin tabulatura, itself rooted in tabula, meaning a board, tablet, or list. First documented in Europe circa 1300 as a means of notating organ music, the system subsequently expanded across centuries to encompass the lute, guitar, and a wide range of other instruments. The abbreviated form, "tab," followed naturally, though neither term adequately describes what notation fundamentally is. Notation is a visual language: a symbolic system designed to communicate musical instruction. The terminology, derived from a Latin word for a writing surface, is nonetheless a poor descriptor for what notation actually achieves as a visual communicative system.

Existing harmonica notation systems present a practical but limited solution. The most common approach uses plain numbers to indicate blow notes, with a minus sign prefix denoting a draw, so "1" means blow on hole 1, while "-1" means draw on hole 1. This is functional, but visually asymmetrical. The draw note at least carries a symbol; the blow note carries none. Direction of airflow for blow notes is communicated by the absence of marking rather than by any visual indicator, which is a weak foundation for an intuitive reading system.

The system proposed here takes a different approach. Each note is rendered as a single triangle glyph: oriented upward to indicate a blow note, downward to indicate a draw, with the corresponding hole number embedded within the shape. Directional instruction and hole position are unified within one symbol. Whether this produces a meaningful improvement in readability remains an open question, and independent evaluation is both welcomed and encouraged. The present document reflects Version 2 of the system, which introduced bend notation absent from the original. Version 3 is intended to incorporate overblows and additional extended techniques. This work is offered as a foundation for further development.
